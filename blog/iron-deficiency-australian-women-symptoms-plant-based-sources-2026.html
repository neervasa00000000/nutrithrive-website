Iron Deficiency in Australian Women: Symptoms (2026)

Roughly 1.1 million Australians are iron deficient, and most don't know it — surveys show the majority of Australians can't identify common iron deficiency symptoms, things like unusual cravings to chew ice, shortness of breath, or low libido, even though it's one of the most common nutritional gaps in the country. Women carry most of that burden, largely due to menstrual blood loss, pregnancy, and diets that fall short of recommended intake.

This isn't a "take more moringa and you'll be fine" post — iron deficiency is a medical condition that needs a blood test to properly diagnose, not a guess based on symptoms. But understanding what's going on, and what plant-based food sources can realistically contribute, is worth a clear explanation.

Why women specifically are at higher risk

A few things stack the odds against Australian women in particular:

Menstrual blood loss is the leading cause of iron deficiency in Australian women — heavy periods in particular significantly increase the risk.

Pregnancy and breastfeeding dramatically increase iron requirements at exactly the time many women are already running low.

Dietary intake gaps. National nutrition data shows Australian women consume noticeably less iron than the recommended daily intake on average, and vegetarians need roughly 80% more dietary iron than meat-eaters do, because plant-based (non-haem) iron is absorbed less efficiently by the body than the iron in red meat.

Under-screening. Iron deficiency testing dropped significantly during the pandemic and many people still aren't routinely screened unless they specifically raise symptoms with a GP.

Symptoms worth taking seriously

Iron deficiency symptoms are easy to write off as "just being busy" or "just getting older" — which is part of why so many people walk around with it unaddressed. Common signs include persistent fatigue and lethargy, difficulty concentrating, irritability, dizziness or headaches, pale skin, cold hands and feet, shortness of breath during normal activity, and less commonly, unusual cravings to chew ice or non-food items.

If several of these sound familiar and persistent, the right next step is a blood test through your GP — specifically ferritin and full blood count — not assuming and self-treating. Iron deficiency without anaemia can still cause real symptoms, and over-supplementing iron without a confirmed deficiency can cause its own problems (constipation, nausea, and in rare cases more serious issues), so testing first matters.

Plant-based iron sources, realistically explained

If a GP has confirmed you're low and recommended boosting dietary iron (alongside or instead of supplements, depending on severity), plant-based iron sources are absolutely part of a real plan — they just work a little differently to iron from meat.

The absorption issue. Iron from plants (non-haem iron) is absorbed at a lower rate than iron from meat, fish, and eggs (haem iron). This is why the recommended intake for vegetarians is set roughly 80% higher than for people who eat meat — it's accounting for that absorption gap, not suggesting plants don't work as a source.

What helps absorption. Pairing plant iron sources with vitamin C significantly improves how much your body actually absorbs — a squeeze of lemon over lentils, capsicum alongside spinach, or citrus in a smoothie with moringa all genuinely help.

What hinders absorption. Tea and coffee taken at the same time as an iron-rich meal can reduce absorption — it's worth leaving a gap of 30–60 minutes either side if iron intake is a focus for you.

Common plant-based iron sources worth knowing:

  • Lentils and legumes
  • Tofu and tempeh
  • Dark leafy greens (spinach, silverbeet)
  • Pumpkin seeds
  • Quinoa
  • Fortified cereals
  • Dried herbs and leaf powders including moringa — naturally iron-dense per serve relative to most leafy greens, particularly once dried and concentrated into powder form

Where moringa fits, honestly

A teaspoon of moringa powder contributes meaningfully to daily iron intake, and it's an easy way to add a plant-based iron source into a smoothie, oats, or yoghurt without changing your whole diet around it. Paired with a vitamin C source (orange, lemon, kiwi) in the same smoothie, you're stacking the conditions for better absorption in one go.

What it isn't: a treatment for diagnosed iron deficiency anaemia, which typically needs a higher, clinically-dosed iron supplement (and sometimes IV iron) prescribed and monitored by a GP. Food sources support overall iron status over time — they're not a substitute for medical treatment of a confirmed deficiency.

Chronic fatigue from low iron often gets mistaken for burnout or "high cortisol" symptoms — they can look identical from the inside. If you're tired, foggy and wired all at once, iron status is worth ruling out before you blame stress specifically. FAQ

How do I know if I'm iron deficient?

The only reliable way is a blood test — specifically serum ferritin alongside a full blood count — ordered by your GP. Symptoms alone (fatigue, dizziness, pale skin) can overlap with many other conditions, so testing is the only way to know for sure.

Is moringa a good source of iron?

Yes, moringa leaf powder is naturally iron-dense, particularly once dried and concentrated, and works as one contributor to overall plant-based iron intake — alongside lentils, leafy greens, and other plant sources, not as a replacement for a varied diet or prescribed treatment.

Why is plant-based iron less effective than meat iron?

Non-haem iron (from plants) is chemically harder for your body to absorb than haem iron (from meat, fish, eggs). Pairing plant iron with vitamin C at the same meal significantly improves absorption and helps close that gap.

Can I just take an iron supplement instead of worrying about food?

Iron supplements should be guided by a blood test and a GP, particularly because over-supplementing iron without a confirmed deficiency can cause side effects and, in some cases, more serious complications. Food-based iron is generally safer to increase without medical supervision than high-dose supplements are.

Are vegetarians and vegans more likely to be iron deficient?

Yes — the recommended iron intake for vegetarians is roughly 80% higher than for those who eat meat, specifically because plant-based iron is absorbed less efficiently, so deliberate attention to iron-rich plant foods and absorption-boosting habits (vitamin C pairing) matters more.
