The Best Plant-Based Iron Foods for Australians (And How to Absorb Them Better)

By Neer, NutriThrive Truganina · Last updated: 7 Jul 2026

Iron deficiency is one of the most common nutritional problems in Australia, and it’s most common in groups who eat less red meat: women of reproductive age, vegetarians and vegans, endurance athletes, and teenagers. If you’re reducing your meat intake or avoiding it entirely, understanding which plant foods deliver meaningful iron, and crucially, how to actually absorb it: is more important than most nutrition guides acknowledge.

Why plant-based iron is different

There are two forms of dietary iron. Haem iron comes from meat, fish, and poultry: it’s absorbed at roughly 15-35% efficiency. Non-haem iron comes from plants: it’s absorbed at only 2-20% efficiency under the same conditions.

This absorption gap is significant, and it’s why the official recommended daily iron intake for vegetarians is set roughly 80% higher than for people who eat meat. It’s not that plant iron doesn’t work: it’s that you need more of it and need to take specific steps to maximise how much your body actually takes up.

The best plant-based iron sources

Lentils: One of the highest plant-based iron sources. A cooked cup delivers around 6.6mg of iron: almost half the daily requirement for a non-vegetarian adult man, and about a third of the higher requirement for vegetarian women. Lentils are also high in protein, fibre, and folate. If you had to pick one iron food to eat regularly, this is it.

Chickpeas and kidney beans: Similar range. Chickpeas provide about 4.7mg per cooked cup; kidney beans slightly more. All legumes are meaningful iron sources and should appear in a vegetarian diet multiple times per week rather than occasionally.

Tofu and tempeh: Soy products are among the most iron-dense plant foods. Tofu at around 3.4mg per 100g and tempeh slightly higher. Both are also significant protein sources, making them doubly useful for plant-based diets.

Pumpkin seeds: One of the most iron-dense single foods per gram. A small handful (28g) delivers about 2.5mg. Easy to add to salads, oats, or smoothies.

Spinach and dark leafy greens: Cooked spinach delivers about 3.6mg per cup, though the phytates and oxalates in raw spinach reduce absorption. Cooking reduces the oxalates and increases the amount absorbed. Swiss chard, silverbeet, and kale are similar.

Moringa leaf powder: Concentrated, dried moringa leaf is iron-dense relative to most leafy greens specifically because the drying concentrates the mineral content. A teaspoon contributes meaningfully to daily iron intake, and pairing it with a vitamin C source in the same meal (lemon in the smoothie, orange juice alongside) amplifies absorption.

Other contributors: Dark chocolate (70%+ cocoa), dried apricots, blackstrap molasses, and fortified cereals (check the label: iron fortification varies widely by brand).

How to actually absorb it

The gap between how much iron a food contains and how much your body uses is large and controllable. The most important factors:

Always pair with vitamin C. This is the single most effective thing you can do. Vitamin C chemically converts non-haem iron to a more absorbable form in your gut. A squeeze of lemon, capsicum in the same meal, or a glass of orange juice with or before your iron-rich food. It doesn’t need to be a large amount, even 25mg (roughly a quarter of a lemon’s juice) makes a meaningful difference.

Avoid tea and coffee with or immediately after iron-rich meals. Tannins in both bind to iron and significantly reduce absorption. Leave a 30-60 minute gap.

Cook with cast iron when possible. A small amount of iron leaches from cast iron cookware into food during cooking: measurable and meaningful, particularly for acidic foods cooked for longer periods.

Be consistent. Dietary iron correction is slow. Unlike supplemental iron which works faster, building iron through food is a weeks-to-months process. Daily consistency matters more than occasional large amounts.

FAQ

Best plant iron foods?

Lentils, chickpeas, tofu, tempeh, pumpkin seeds, spinach, moringa leaf powder, dark chocolate.

How can vegetarians get enough?

80% higher intake than meat-eaters, paired with vitamin C, avoiding tea/coffee around meals.

Does vitamin C really help?

Yes. One of the best-established nutrition pairings. Can increase absorption 2-6 times.
