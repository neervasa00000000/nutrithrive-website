Introduction: Why curry leaves are becoming popular in Australia

Walk into any authentic South Indian kitchen and you’ll probably hear the crackle of curry leaves hitting hot oil. That aroma — fresh, citrusy, slightly nutty — is one of the defining flavours of many Indian dishes.

In Australia, interest in curry leaves has grown quickly over the last decade. As Indian, Sri Lankan and Malaysian cuisine become more common in Melbourne, Sydney and Brisbane, home cooks are looking for ways to recreate those flavours at home.

The challenge? Fresh curry leaves can be difficult to find.

While some Indian grocery stores stock them occasionally, they are not always available in mainstream supermarkets. Because of this, many Australian cooks now keep dried curry leaves in their pantry as a reliable alternative.

For cooks in Melbourne, Sydney and Brisbane, dried curry leaves have become a convenient pantry staple when fresh leaves are difficult to find.

They last much longer, are easier to store, and still provide the distinctive aroma that makes curry leaves so special. Whether you are making dal, rice dishes, chutneys or herbal teas, dried curry leaves can bring authentic flavour to everyday cooking.

What are curry leaves?

Curry leaves come from the tropical plant Murraya koenigii, which is native to India and Sri Lanka. The leaves are widely used across South Asian cooking and are valued for their aromatic oils and distinctive flavour.

Despite the name, curry leaves have nothing to do with curry powder.

Curry powder is a spice blend that may contain turmeric, cumin, coriander, fenugreek and other spices. Curry leaves, on the other hand, are a fresh ingredient used to flavour dishes during cooking.

Their flavour is difficult to describe precisely, but many cooks say it combines notes of:

  • citrus
  • toasted nuts
  • herbs
  • subtle bitterness

When fried briefly in oil, curry leaves release essential oils that perfume an entire dish. That’s why they are often used early in cooking, especially in a technique called tempering.

Curry leaves are used widely in:

  • Indian cuisine
  • Sri Lankan cooking
  • Malaysian and Singaporean dishes
  • South Asian spice blends

While fresh leaves provide the strongest aroma, dried leaves are much easier to store and are increasingly popular among Australian home cooks.

Fresh vs dried curry leaves

Fresh curry leaves are usually considered the gold standard in traditional cooking. However, dried curry leaves offer a practical alternative, especially for people who don’t have access to fresh leaves regularly.

Here’s a simple comparison:

FeatureFresh leavesDried leaves
Shelf lifeAround 1 week6–12 months
FlavorStrong and vibrantSlightly milder
AvailabilityLimited outside specialty storesWidely available
ConvenienceRequires refrigerationPantry friendly

The main difference is intensity. Fresh leaves contain more natural oils, so their flavour is stronger. However, dried leaves still provide a similar aroma when cooked properly.

A simple rule many cooks follow is:

Use two to three times more dried curry leaves than fresh ones.

For example, if a recipe calls for 10 fresh leaves, you might use around 20–25 dried leaves instead.

Dried leaves are particularly useful for everyday cooking because they last longer and are always ready to use.

Dried curry leaves uses: what to do with them in the kitchen

One of the most common questions from Australian home cooks is how to use dried curry leaves — or even how to use dry curry leaves if you're typing quickly. The answer starts with understanding that dried curry leaves need heat and oil to release their flavour. Just tossing them into a dish raw won't do much. Below are the main dried curry leaves uses and techniques, plus simple recipes you can try tonight.

Tempering (tadka)

Tempering — also known as tadka — is the single most important technique for cooking with curry leaves. Whole spices and curry leaves are briefly fried in hot oil or ghee. This releases their aroma and creates a flavour base for the dish.

A typical tempering might include:

  • 1 tsp mustard seeds
  • 1 tsp cumin seeds
  • 2 dried red chillies, snapped in half
  • 2 cloves garlic, sliced
  • 15–20 dried curry leaves

Heat 2 tablespoons of oil or ghee in a small pan over medium-high heat. When the oil is hot, add the mustard seeds. When they start to pop, add the cumin, chillies, garlic, and curry leaves all at once. Let them sizzle for 15–20 seconds — the leaves should crisp and release their fragrance — then immediately pour the entire contents over your dal, vegetables, or rice. Do not let the leaves burn or they turn bitter.

Dal and lentil dishes

Curry leaves are a key ingredient in many lentil dishes like dal and sambar. When dried curry leaves are used, fry them first in oil to release their flavour, then add to the cooked lentils. The aromatic oils complement the earthy richness of lentils beautifully.

Curry leaf rice

A simple but flavourful dish from South India:

  • Cook 1 cup basmati rice and spread on a plate to cool
  • Heat 2 tbsp oil, add 1 tsp mustard seeds, 1 tsp urad dal, 1 dried chilli, and 20 dried curry leaves
  • When the seeds pop and leaves crisp (about 20 seconds), add ½ tsp turmeric, a pinch of salt, and 2 tbsp roasted peanuts
  • Fold in the cooled rice and squeeze lime juice over the top

Chutney powder (karuveppilai podi)

In South Indian cooking, roasted curry leaves are sometimes ground with lentils, chillies and spices to create a dry chutney powder called karuveppilai podi. This powder can be sprinkled over rice with ghee or used as a condiment. To make it: dry-roast ¼ cup curry leaves, 2 tbsp urad dal, 2 tbsp chana dal, 2 dried chillies, and a pinch of asafoetida until fragrant. Grind to a coarse powder with salt to taste.

Curry leaf tea

What to do with dried curry leaves beyond cooking? Some people steep 8–10 dried leaves in hot water for 5 minutes to make a mild herbal tea. The flavour is subtle and slightly savoury. It's sometimes consumed for digestive comfort, though the evidence is traditional rather than clinical.

Quick potato curry with dried curry leaves

A 15-minute recipe that shows off the leaves perfectly:

  • Boil 3 medium potatoes, peel and cube them
  • Heat 2 tbsp oil, add 1 tsp mustard seeds and 15 dried curry leaves
  • When seeds pop, add ½ tsp turmeric, ½ tsp chilli powder, and a pinch of salt
  • Toss in the potato cubes and stir to coat — cook 3 minutes until lightly golden
  • Squeeze lime over the top and serve

Health benefits of curry leaves

Beyond their culinary value, curry leaves contain a range of beneficial plant compounds. Research into Murraya koenigii has identified several bioactive constituents, including carbazole alkaloids, which are being studied for antioxidant and anti-inflammatory properties.

Some potential benefits associated with curry leaves include:

  • Antioxidant activity: curry leaves contain carbazole alkaloids and flavonoids that help neutralise free radicals and reduce oxidative stress in cells
  • Digestive support: traditionally used in Ayurveda to stimulate digestive enzymes and ease mild stomach discomfort
  • Blood sugar regulation: some animal studies suggest curry leaf extracts may help moderate blood glucose levels, though human evidence is still limited
  • Anti-inflammatory compounds: the same carbazole alkaloids show anti-inflammatory activity in laboratory settings
  • Iron content: curry leaves provide a modest amount of dietary iron, which supports red blood cell formation and energy metabolism
  • Hair and skin health: in traditional Indian practice, curry leaf oil is applied to hair to support scalp health — this is folk use, not clinical evidence

In traditional medicine systems such as Ayurveda, curry leaves have long been used to support digestion and metabolic health. However, curry leaves are primarily a culinary ingredient, not a medical treatment. They can be part of a healthy diet but should not be relied upon as a cure for health conditions.

How to store dried curry leaves

Proper storage helps preserve the aroma and flavour of dried curry leaves.

Because the leaves contain natural oils, they can lose their fragrance if exposed to air, heat or moisture for long periods.

To keep them fresh:

  • Use an airtight container. Glass jars or sealed pouches work best.
  • Store in a cool, dry place. Avoid placing them near the stove or oven.
  • Protect them from sunlight. Light can break down the aromatic oils.
  • Keep them dry. Moisture can cause clumping or spoilage.

When stored properly, dried curry leaves can last six to twelve months while still retaining their flavour.

Melbourne kitchens: keep jars away from the cooktop steam zone; decant from soft plastic into glass if the pouch zipper fails—humidity is what mutes aroma fastest.

Common cooking mistakes

Even experienced cooks sometimes make mistakes when using curry leaves.

Here are a few common ones:

  • Adding them too late in cooking. Curry leaves release flavour when heated, so they should usually be added early.
  • Not frying them first. Tempering the leaves in oil helps release their essential oils.
  • Using too few dried leaves. Dried leaves are milder than fresh ones, so recipes often need more.
  • Improper storage. Leaving curry leaves exposed to air or sunlight can quickly reduce their aroma.

Avoiding these mistakes can dramatically improve the flavour of your dishes.
