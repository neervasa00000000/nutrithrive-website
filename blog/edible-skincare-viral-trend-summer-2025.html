The "Internal Retinol" Connection

You've likely heard of Retinol. It's the gold standard for anti-ageing because it speeds up cell turnover. But synthetic retinol can be harsh, causing redness and peeling—especially in the harsh Australian sun.

Here is the secret: Vitamin A is the natural precursor to retinol. Your body uses Vitamin A to repair skin tissue and keep it firm.

Moringa leaves contain roughly 10x more Vitamin A than carrots.

When you drink NutriThrive Moringa, you are essentially consuming a gentle, natural "internal retinol." You are flooding your system with the exact nutrient your skin needs to repair itself, without the harsh side effects of topical acids.

Why You Need "Sun Food" This Summer

As we head into a hot Australian summer, UV damage is the enemy. UV rays cause "oxidative stress" (rusting of the cells), which leads to fine lines and pigmentation.

You can wear sunscreen (and you should!), but sunscreen only protects the surface.

To protect the deeper layers of your skin, you need Antioxidants. Moringa has one of the highest antioxidant scores (ORAC value) of any food on earth—higher than acai, matcha, or blueberries.

Think of it as an internal shield. While sunscreen blocks the rays on the outside, the antioxidants in NutriThrive Moringa help neutralise the free radicals that manage to get through.

Frequently Asked Questions About Moringa

Moringa leaves are rich in vitamin C, vitamin A precursors, calcium, iron, and plant antioxidants, which may support overall nutrition, blood sugar and cholesterol control, and anti-inflammatory activity in people with poor diets. "Superfood" or "miracle tree" are marketing-style labels; human clinical data show modest benefits (for example, small improvements in blood sugar or lipids) rather than dramatic cures.

Leaf powder contains protein, fiber, vitamins A, C, E, B vitamins, calcium, potassium, iron, and phytochemicals like flavonoids and glucosinolates; content varies by source and processing. Tea made from leaves mainly provides some antioxidants and small amounts of micronutrients, not full meal-level nutrition.

Powder has an earthy, slightly bitter, "green" flavor often compared with spinach, matcha, or fenugreek, and can be strong if used in large quantities.

Common uses include adding 1–2 teaspoons to smoothies, yogurt, soups, stews, or baking, or taking as capsules; it is usually not heated for long at high temperatures to preserve some nutrients.

Small food-like amounts (for example, a teaspoon of powder in food) are generally considered acceptable for healthy adults, but there is no standard "must take" recommendation, and long-term high-dose safety is not well defined. Use is optional, not essential; if you do take it daily, choose reputable brands and monitor for side effects like digestive upset or changes in labs.

Tablets and other supplements are similar to capsules; they may support nutrient intake but should not replace a varied diet or prescribed treatment. Safety depends on dose, product quality, and your health status; people with thyroid disease, on multiple medications, pregnant, or with liver/kidney issues should seek professional guidance.
