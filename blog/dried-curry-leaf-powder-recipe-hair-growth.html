The "Green Gold" Recipe (Takes 2 Minutes)

This "Gunpowder" sprinkle tastes amazing on avocado toast, eggs, rice, or even mixed into yogurt. It's a versatile condiment that packs a nutritional punch.

Ingredients:

  • 1 Cup NutriThrive Dried Curry Leaves (You need a whole bag!)
  • 2 tbsp Sesame Seeds (Calcium boost for hair strength)
  • 1 tsp Black Pepper (Helps with nutrient absorption)
  • Salt to taste (optional, but enhances flavor)

Method:

  1. Dry roast the sesame seeds and black pepper in a pan over low heat for 1 minute until fragrant. Be careful not to burn them.
  2. Add the dried curry leaves for just 10 seconds to warm them up and release their essential oils. Don't overheat or they'll lose their nutrients.
  3. Let everything cool completely before grinding (this prevents the powder from clumping).
  4. Blitz everything in a blender or spice grinder until you get a fine powder. You may need to pulse it a few times to get the right consistency.
  5. Store in an airtight jar in a cool, dark place. The powder will last for 3-6 months.

ðŸ’¡ How to Use: Eat 1 teaspoon daily. Sprinkle it on rice, mix it into eggs, add it to avocado toast, or stir it into yogurt. The roasted sesame and pepper make it taste like a savoury spice mix, not a leaf.

This recipe uses a full cup of dried curry leaves, which is why having a quality supply is essential. NutriThrive's shade-dried leaves are perfect for this because they retain maximum nutrients and flavor.

Why This Works for Hair Growth

Eating curry leaf powder works from the inside out, providing your hair follicles with the nutrients they need to grow strong, healthy hair:

  • Iron: Dried curry leaves are packed with concentrated iron (ferritin), which is essential for stopping hair fall. Iron deficiency is one of the leading causes of hair loss.
  • Amino Acids: The building blocks of hair protein. Curry leaves contain all essential amino acids needed for hair growth.
  • Beta-Carotene: Converts to Vitamin A, which promotes healthy hair growth and prevents premature greying.
  • Antioxidants: Protect hair follicles from damage and oxidative stress.
  • No "Grassy" Taste: The roasted sesame and pepper make it taste like a savoury spice mix, making it easy to incorporate into your daily diet.

Unlike topical treatments that only work on the surface, eating curry leaf powder nourishes your hair from the roots, providing sustained benefits over time.

The "Lazy" Tea Option

Curry Leaf Tea Recipe:

  1. Take 10 dried curry leaves
  2. Crush them lightly in your palm to break the cell walls
  3. Steep in hot water for 5 minutes
  4. Strain and drink on an empty stomach

Benefits: Flushes toxins, supports digestion, and provides a gentle energy boost. Dried curry leaves steep better than fresh because the cell walls are already broken, releasing more nutrients into the water.

This tea is particularly popular for morning detox routines and weight management. The dried curry leaves release their beneficial compounds more effectively when steeped, making it an easy way to get your daily dose of nutrients.

Frequently Asked Questions About Moringa

Moringa leaves are rich in vitamin C, vitamin A precursors, calcium, iron, and plant antioxidants, which may support overall nutrition, blood sugar and cholesterol control, and anti-inflammatory activity in people with poor diets. "Superfood" or "miracle tree" are marketing-style labels; human clinical data show modest benefits (for example, small improvements in blood sugar or lipids) rather than dramatic cures.

Leaf powder contains protein, fiber, vitamins A, C, E, B vitamins, calcium, potassium, iron, and phytochemicals like flavonoids and glucosinolates; content varies by source and processing. Tea made from leaves mainly provides some antioxidants and small amounts of micronutrients, not full meal-level nutrition.

Powder has an earthy, slightly bitter, "green" flavor often compared with spinach, matcha, or fenugreek, and can be strong if used in large quantities.

Common uses include adding 1â€“2 teaspoons to smoothies, yogurt, soups, stews, or baking, or taking as capsules; it is usually not heated for long at high temperatures to preserve some nutrients.

Small food-like amounts (for example, a teaspoon of powder in food) are generally considered acceptable for healthy adults, but there is no standard "must take" recommendation, and long-term high-dose safety is not well defined.
