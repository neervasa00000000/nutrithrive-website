Moringa is grown in warmer parts of Australia such as tropical and subtropical regions; in cooler cities like Melbourne it may survive only in sheltered microclimates, in pots that can be moved indoors, or as a warm-season plant that may defoliate or die back in winter.

Some small human trials report reductions in total and LDL cholesterol with leaf preparations, but data are still limited and not a replacement for statins or other prescribed therapy.

Leaves are small, oval, and grow in feather-like clusters on a central stem, giving a light, airy look. They taste mildly bitter, nutty, and spinach-like, especially when cooked.

Crushed moringa seeds contain positively charged proteins that bind to negatively charged particles and microbes in water, helping them clump and settle out; this is used as a low-tech clarification method in some areas.

Powder is produced in many moringa-growing countries (for example, India, parts of Africa, Southeast Asia) and exported globally; it is used in health foods, fortified meals, and supplements.

Fine plant fibers and hydrophobic components can make powder clump and float; vigorous shaking or blending helps suspend it more evenly.

Capsules can provide concentrated leaf powder with antioxidants and micronutrients, but typical doses contribute modest nutrients compared with whole foods, and high-dose or long-term safety data are limited.
