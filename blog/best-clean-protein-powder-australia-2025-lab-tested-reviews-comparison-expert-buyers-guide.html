Frequently Asked Questions

Is plant-based protein as good as whey?

Yes! With the right blend (pea, pumpkin, moringa), you get all essential amino acids, no dairy, and much easier digestion. Plant-based proteins are often better for those with sensitive stomachs and provide additional nutrients like iron, calcium, and antioxidants that whey doesn't offer.

What does "lab-tested" mean?

The protein is checked for purity, amino acid content, and absence of contaminantsâ€”look for published results. Reputable brands will share their 3rd-party lab results showing heavy metal testing, amino acid profiles, and purity verification.

How much protein do I need per serving?

Most people benefit from 20-30g of protein per serving. For muscle building and recovery, aim for 25-30g. For general wellness, 15-20g is sufficient. Always check the nutrition label and adjust based on your individual needs and goals.

Can I use clean protein powder for weight loss?

Yes! Clean protein powders are excellent for weight loss because they're low in calories, help maintain muscle mass, and keep you feeling full longer. Choose unflavoured or naturally flavoured options to avoid added sugars.

What's the difference between clean protein and regular protein powder?

Clean protein powder uses premium, tested sources with no artificial additives, sweeteners, or fillers. Regular protein powders often contain artificial flavours, gums, excess sugars, and may not be lab-tested for purity. Clean protein prioritizes transparency and quality.

Frequently Asked Questions About Moringa

Moringa leaves are rich in vitamin C, vitamin A precursors, calcium, iron, and plant antioxidants, which may support overall nutrition, blood sugar and cholesterol control, and anti-inflammatory activity in people with poor diets. "Superfood" or "miracle tree" are marketing-style labels; human clinical data show modest benefits (for example, small improvements in blood sugar or lipids) rather than dramatic cures.

Leaf powder contains protein, fiber, vitamins A, C, E, B vitamins, calcium, potassium, iron, and phytochemicals like flavonoids and glucosinolates; content varies by source and processing. Tea made from leaves mainly provides some antioxidants and small amounts of micronutrients, not full meal-level nutrition.

Powder has an earthy, slightly bitter, "green" flavor often compared with spinach, matcha, or fenugreek, and can be strong if used in large quantities.

Common uses include adding 1â€“2 teaspoons to smoothies, yogurt, soups, stews, or baking, or taking as capsules; it is usually not heated for long at high temperatures to preserve some nutrients.

Small food-like amounts (for example, a teaspoon of powder in food) are generally considered acceptable for healthy adults, but there is no standard "must take" recommendation, and long-term high-dose safety is not well defined. Use is optional, not essential; if you do take it daily, choose reputable brands and monitor for side effects like digestive upset or changes in labs.
