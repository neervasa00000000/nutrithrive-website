What is kari leaf? (Murraya koenigii)

Kari leaf — also sold as dried curry leaves — grows on the curry leaf tree (Murraya koenigii). In the wild, this small tropical tree is native to India and common across the Indian subcontinent, including Sri Lanka. It belongs to the rue family and is sometimes called the curry bush in gardening guides. Do not confuse it with “curry plant” (Helichrysum italicum), which is a different herb entirely.

On the tree, mature plants produce clusters of small white or white flowers that later form dark berries. Cooks use only the glossy leaves. The flavour is citrusy and nutty — nothing like blended curry powder. That is why authentic South Asian recipes call for whole leaves at the start of cooking, not ground spice mix.

How to use dried kari leaf

Step 1 — Measure: Use about two to three times more dried leaf than you would fresh.

Step 2 — Temper: Fry leaves in hot oil, ghee, or coconut oil until crisp and fragrant (30–60 seconds). This is the classic tadka step in Indian cuisine.

Step 3 — Build the dish: Add onions, spices, or wet ingredients next. The infused oil carries the aroma through the recipe.

Storage: Keep the pouch sealed, away from heat and sunlight. Most households get six months or more of good flavour.

Frequently Asked Questions

Is kari leaf the same as curry powder?

No. Kari leaf (Murraya koenigii) is a whole herb from the curry leaf tree. Curry powder is a ground blend of many spices. They are used differently in South Asian cooking.

How do dried curry leaves compare to fresh?

Dried leaves are easier to store and ship. Use about two to three times the amount of dried leaf compared with fresh for a similar aroma in the finished dish.

What dishes can I use them in?

Use them in tadka for dal, sambar, rasam, lemon rice, potato curry, and coconut-based gravies. They suit any recipe that calls for curry leaf in Indian or Sri Lankan-style cooking.

How should I store them?

Seal the bag or move leaves to an airtight jar. Store in a cool cupboard away from the stove. Properly stored, flavour stays strong for six months or longer.
